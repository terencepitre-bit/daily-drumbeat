[ P9 · HEALTH EQUITY · BRIEFING ]

Mississippi hospital closure fuels maternal health crisis

HEALTH EQUITY — After her husband raced her 25 miles to a hospital during a preeclampsia emergency, Harmony Ball-Stribling died on the roadside in 2021 when the local hospital in Belzoni, Mississippi had closed thirteen years earlier. Today, Humphreys County has no hospital, no emergency room, and no urgent care clinic. Black women are nearly three times more likely to die due to childbirth than white women in Mississippi. Recent state legislation exempting Humphreys County from certificate-of-need requirements could attract healthcare providers, but financial challenges remain.

The takeaway
Mississippi's continued rejection of Medicaid expansion blocks critical rural healthcare investment when Black maternal mortality disparities demand urgent action.

[ P10 · LAND & LEGACY · BRIEFING ]

Highway expansion threatens Black land, property, and safety

LAND & LEGACY — Black residents across the South say new highway projects are flooding their land and eroding soil after recent widening projects; in Shiloh, Alabama, flooding from a highway project has cost residents thousands and stripped property insurance, even as a massive new 52-mile highway threatens more Black communities near Birmingham. In Charlotte, the Black Political Caucus filed a lawsuit in March 2026 to halt the $3.2 billion I-77 expansion that would add toll lanes from Uptown to South Carolina.

The takeaway
Decades after highway construction devastated Black neighborhoods, new expansions repeat the same pattern of displacement and environmental harm.
Sources [Capital B News] [African Elements]
[ P2 · POLICY & JUSTICE · BRIEFING ]

Chemical companies block toxic air monitoring in Louisiana

POLICY & JUSTICE — Dow Chemical's Plaquemine plastics factory, one of Louisiana's biggest air polluters releasing the carcinogen ethylene oxide, sits 8 miles from the nearest state air monitor, which measures only ozone; the three chemicals researchers say cause the largest cancer risk in the area—ethylene oxide, chloroprene, and formaldehyde—aren't monitored by the state at all. Industry groups have spent years blocking fenceline monitoring proposals and helped draft a 2024 law restricting how community air monitoring data can be used in enforcement.

The takeaway
Louisiana families breathe unmonitored toxic air while chemical companies use political power to block health protections.
Sources [Capital B News] [The Lens NOLA]
