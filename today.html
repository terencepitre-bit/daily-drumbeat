George E. Johnson, Black Business Pioneer, Dies at 99

George E. Johnson, who created a Black hair care empire, died July 6 at age 99. He founded Johnson Products Company in 1954 and turned it into a multimillion-dollar business; in 1971 it became the first Black-owned company to trade on the American Stock Exchange. Those who knew him noted that many Chicago Black-owned businesses may not exist today without Johnson's mentorship, as he taught entrepreneurs about business structures and operations.

Sources [Chicago Sun-Times] [ABC7 Chicago]

Black Women Disappearing From Labor Force Despite Rate Drop

From March to July 2026, Black women's unemployment rate fell from 7.07% to 5.73%, appearing to show progress, but underneath that improvement their labor-market position deteriorated. While the working-age population of Black women grew by 67,000, employment among Black women fell by 212,000. When Black women begin disappearing from the labor force, the economy is not becoming stronger—it is losing capacity.

Sources [Fortune] [Joint Center for Political and Economic Studies]
[ P5 · HBCUS & EDUCATION ]

HBCU Enrollment Boom: Spelman Becomes Highly Selective

Spelman College grew its enrollment by more than 20 percent between 2020 and 2025, while the acceptance rate declined from about 50 percent to 19 percent. For the first time in recent history, Black students around the country are choosing to attend historically Black colleges and universities in record numbers, with enrollment numbers suggesting many HBCUs are giving selective institutions a run for their money. Spelman experienced a 17.7 percent increase in early applications for Fall 2026 enrollment.

Sources [The Root] [The Journal of Blacks in Higher Education]
[ P11 · BLACK EXCELLENCE ]

2026 BET Awards Honor Black Excellence Across Generations

The 2026 BET Awards highlighted lineage and mentorship, showing Janet Jackson honoring Teyana Taylor, Lauryn Hill watching artists she influenced reinterpret her work, and Sylvia Rhone being celebrated for helping others enter powerful rooms. Veteran music executive Sylvia Rhone received the Ultimate Icon Award for a groundbreaking career spanning approximately 50 years, and became the first Black woman to occupy numerous top-level roles within the recording industry.
